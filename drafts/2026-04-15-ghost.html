The lead story today is the active Special Marine Warning. Away from the warning zone, observed buoy data paints a picture of moderate, manageable conditions. Buoy 41009, roughly 20 nm off the Space Coast, is showing 4.9 ft seas at a 10-second period with light 5.8 kt east winds. The longer period swell means these waves are organized rollers, not chop, and bottom sediment disturbance will be greater than the height alone suggests. Closer in, buoy 41114 (6.5 nm off the Treasure Coast) shows 4.3 ft seas at 10 seconds and 77.2°F water. The Lake Worth sensor at Blue Heron Bridge and Singer Island is reading 14.0 kt ESE wind and 76.8°F water. South of the warning, buoy 41122 (23 nm offshore near Deerfield through Miami) reads 3.3 ft seas at a short 5-second period and 78.1°F water. That short-period chop stays near the surface and is less disruptive to bottom visibility than the longer swell to the north.

🚨 Safety Tip

Waterspouts can form and dissipate within minutes.

Today's Special Marine Warning was radar-indicated, meaning the threat was identified by NWS before mariners on the water could see it visually. If you are in a warning zone, do not wait to see a waterspout before acting. Waterspouts can reach wind speeds of 50 kt or more and produce sudden wave height increases that can capsize small craft. Return to port immediately when a warning is issued, monitor VHF weather channel WX1, and do not resume activity until NWS issues an all-clear. Always file a float plan before departure so someone ashore knows your expected return.
