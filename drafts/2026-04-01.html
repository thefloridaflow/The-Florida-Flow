Conditions Headline

A strong shower is moving westward across the Atlantic waters east of South Florida this afternoon, with Doppler radar indicating winds near 30 knots as of 4:09 PM EDT. The Space Coast (buoy 41009, 20 nm offshore) is observing 4.9 ft seas with a 9-second period and 11.7 kt ESE wind—the roughest surf measured along the entire coast today. The Treasure Coast (buoy 41114, 6.5 nm offshore) shows calmer conditions at 3.9 ft with the same 9-second period, while South Florida from Deerfield to Miami (buoy 41122, 23 nm offshore) is reporting 3.0 ft seas with shorter 5-second periods and warmer water at 77.4°F. The Upper Keys (buoy 42095, 15 nm offshore) match South Florida with 3.0 ft seas and 77.0°F water temperature.

The National Weather Service has issued a Marine Advisory for coastal and nearshore waters from Jupiter Inlet to Ocean Reef FL out 20 nm, as well as offshore zones from 20–60 nm. The active shower, located 13–14 nm east of Lighthouse Point, is moving west at 15 knots. Mariners should expect gusty winds locally exceeding 30 knots, higher waves, lightning, heavy downpours, and the possibility of waterspouts. Boaters are urged to seek safe harbor until this activity clears.

Narcosis Dive Shop (West Palm Beach) confirmed dive conditions on March 28 with 70–80 ft visibility, 75°F water temperature, calm current, and 1 ft wave conditions at their location. Rainbow Reef (Key Largo) has not yet logged conditions for today.

Blue Heron Bridge tide windows are tight today: high tide at 08:37 AM (2.7 ft) with a good dive window from 8:07–9:07 AM, and again at 21:05 (2.95 ft) for a fair evening window from 8:35–9:35 PM. East winds at 14.0 kt are present at BHB. Tomorrow's morning window is slightly later at 09:16 AM (2.6 ft, good) and the evening window at 21:45 (2.97 ft, fair).

Daily Safety Tip
Waterspout Alert: Know the Signs
A strong shower with gusty winds and waterspout potential is moving westward across Atlantic waters today. Waterspouts can develop rapidly with little warning. If you are on the water, watch for a rotating column of water or a dark funnel descending from the clouds — immediately move perpendicular to the storm's path and head for safe harbor. If you hear sirens or see a cone funnel, radio the Coast Guard and get off the water. Boaters are urged to seek shelter now until the shower clears completely.
