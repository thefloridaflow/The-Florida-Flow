Conditions Headline

A strong shower with damaging wind gusts around 30 knots is moving west and affecting coastal and offshore waters from Jupiter Inlet to Ocean Reef, with the heaviest activity currently 13 nautical miles east of Hillsboro Beach. Seas across South Florida range from 2.6 ft in the Upper Keys to 4.6 ft on the Space Coast and Treasure Coast (offshore buoy readings 20 nm out), with wind picking up to 17 knots today across central Palm Beach and the Intracoastal. Water temperatures are holding warm: 76–77°F across the region.

The National Weather Service (AMZ630, issued this morning) issued a strong shower alert affecting all coastal waters from Jupiter Inlet to Ocean Reef, out to 60 nm. The shower cell was located 13 nm east of Hillsboro Beach at 4:09 PM EDT, moving west at 15 knots. Mariners can expect gusty winds to around 30 knots, locally higher waves, lightning, and heavy downpours. Waterspouts are possible with this activity. This is an active marine alert; boaters must remain on alert and seek shelter if the warning advances to a formal Small Craft Advisory or Gale Warning.

Narcosis Dive in West Palm Beach reported excellent conditions yesterday (April 1): 50–60 ft visibility, water at 77°F, light north current, 3–4 ft waves, and sightings of tropical fish, a hawksbill turtle, spotted and green morays, and vibrant corals and sponges.

Blue Heron Bridge has two dive windows today: a good window at 09:16 AM (2.6 ft tide, 8:46 AM–9:46 AM) and a fair window at 09:45 PM (3.0 ft tide, 9:15 PM–10:15 PM). Incoming tide at 09:16 AM is optimal. Morning entry is recommended given current wind and storm activity.

Shower & Waterspout Awareness — Seek Immediate Shelter
A strong rain shower with wind gusts around 30 knots is currently moving west toward South Florida and is affecting coastal waters from Jupiter Inlet to Ocean Reef. The National Weather Service has indicated that waterspouts are possible with this activity. If you are on the water, monitor marine alerts continuously, seek safe harbor immediately, and do not venture back out until the shower has passed and visibility, wind, and sea conditions have stabilized.
